## EMAIL 2 — Day 2

**Subject:** Why checking your phone first thing is costing you the entire morning

**Preview text:** The 23-minute window most fathers waste without knowing it exists.

---

There is a 23-minute window after you wake up that determines the quality of your entire morning. Most people — most fathers especially — give it away without realising it.

Here is what is happening in your body.

**The cortisol awakening response (CAR)** is a 50–75% spike in cortisol that occurs in the first 20–30 minutes after waking. It is your body's natural activation system. It drives alertness, focus, and motivation for the first half of your day.

This is not optional biology. It happens whether you use it or not.

The problem: **your phone hijacks it.**

When you check your phone in the first 20 minutes — email, news, social media, even the weather — you hand the CAR's energy to someone else's agenda. The cortisol spike is consumed by reactive input instead of proactive intention. The result is that groggy, scattered, already-behind feeling that most people attribute to "not being a morning person."

You are a morning person. You are just giving the morning away before it starts.

**The fix is mechanical, not motivational:**

For the first 20 minutes after waking, the phone stays in another room. Not on silent. Not face-down. In another room.

What you do instead matters less than what you do not do. But if you want a structure: one glass of water, one minute of movement (stretching, walking to the kitchen), and one sentence written in a notebook — what matters today?

That is it. Three things. Under five minutes. The remaining 15 minutes of the CAR window can be whatever you want — just not your phone.

Try it for three mornings. Not seven. Three. That is enough to feel the difference.

## EMAIL 3 — Day 4

**Subject:** It is not a discipline problem — it is a design problem

**Preview text:** Why building habits as a new father is structurally harder than it is for everyone else.

---

If you have tried to build a routine as a new or expectant father and it has not stuck — I want to tell you something that the self-improvement world will not.

It is not a discipline problem. It is a design problem.

Here is why.

Every habit-building framework you have ever read — Atomic Habits, the 21-day rule, morning routine YouTube videos — assumes three things: **consistent sleep, predictable mornings, and a stable identity.** New and expectant fathers have none of these.

Your sleep is fragmented or about to be. Your mornings are interrupted or unpredictable. And your identity — who you are, what you value, what your days look like — is undergoing the largest restructure of your adult life.

Building habits on this foundation is like renovating a house during an earthquake. The tools work. The foundation does not.

**This is not a personal failing. It is a structural mismatch.**

The frameworks are not wrong. They are built for a different person in a different situation. What you need is a system designed for instability — one that assumes the worst day, not the best.

That means:

**Shorter protocols.** If your morning routine requires 60 minutes of uninterrupted time, it will fail by week two. Fifteen minutes is the upper limit. Ten is better.

**Fewer decisions.** Every choice you make on low sleep depletes a resource you do not have to spare. The routine should be so specific that you do not have to think — you just follow the sequence.

**Failure-proof defaults.** When the routine breaks — and it will — there should be a minimum viable version that takes three minutes. Not a restart. A fallback.

This is what NextRoutine is built around. Not the best-case morning. The realistic one. The one where the baby woke up twice and you have been vertical for 11 minutes and you are already behind.

That father still deserves a system.
