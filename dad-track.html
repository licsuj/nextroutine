Common questions

Dad Track questions, answered directly

New dads need a radically reduced version of their previous morning routine — not an abandoned one. The 15-Minute New Dad Morning Protocol is built around the reality that you have 15 minutes maximum, possibly fewer, and that sleep deprivation is real. The protocol focuses on three things only: physical activation (5 minutes), one intentional thought (2 minutes), and one boundary-setting action before the day's demands begin (8 minutes). It's designed to be done in whatever fragment of time appears — not a fixed window.
Beyond the nursery and the nappy changing practice, three areas matter most: sleep banking (building as much reserve as possible in the weeks before birth), habit audit (which current habits need to be redesigned for the new reality rather than abandoned), and identity preparation (fatherhood is an identity shift, not just a schedule change). The version of yourself you want your child to see needs to start developing before they arrive — not after.
Managing severe sleep deprivation is about damage limitation, not optimisation. The Dad Track Sleep Survival Protocol focuses on: sleep banking before birth, strategic napping (20-minute naps are more restorative than 40-minute ones due to sleep cycle structure), shift-based sleep agreements with your partner, and the specific nutrition and movement habits that increase resilience to sleep deprivation. The goal in the first 8 weeks isn't to thrive — it's to function, stay present, and not make decisions you'll regret.
The best morning routine for a new father is the one that survives contact with a newborn. That means: flexible timing (your morning routine happens when the baby gives you a window, not at 5am on a schedule), modular structure (three independent modules you can do in any order), minimum viable version (the one habit you'd do even on the worst day), and explicit acknowledgement that "good enough" in the newborn phase is an achievement, not a failure.
Yes — the Dad Track is specifically designed to begin before birth. Stage 1 ("The News") starts from the moment you find out, with an identity audit and pre-baby habit design. Stage 2 ("The Wait") covers the pregnancy months with sleep banking protocols, financial routine resets, and relationship maintenance systems. Starting before the baby arrives means you're building infrastructure rather than reacting to chaos when it hits.
