The science

What's actually happening when you can't sleep

Not woo. Not generic sleep hygiene tips. The specific mechanisms your phone is exploiting.

💡

Blue Light Suppresses Melatonin

Phone screens emit blue light at 480nm — the exact wavelength that tells your pineal gland to stop producing melatonin. Within 30 minutes of screen use, melatonin production drops by up to 50%. Your body doesn't know it's nighttime. So you don't feel tired when you should.

😤

Social Media Raises Cortisol

Every notification, comparison, and piece of outrage content your feed serves spikes cortisol — your stress hormone. Cortisol and melatonin are inversely linked. High cortisol at bedtime means you can't physically relax into sleep, even when you feel exhausted.

🔄

Dopamine Loops Delay Sleep Onset

The variable reward mechanism in social feeds — the same one slot machines use — keeps your dopamine system in anticipation mode. Your brain literally cannot shift into sleep mode while it's waiting for the next hit. "Just one more scroll" is a neurological trap, not a choice.

Common questions

Sleep questions, answered honestly

The single most effective change most people can make is stopping phone use 60–90 minutes before bed. Blue light suppresses melatonin by up to 50%, signalling to your brain that it's still daytime. Beyond that, a consistent sleep and wake time — even on weekends — is the most powerful circadian reset available. A structured wind-down of just 20 minutes (dim lights, no screens, light stretching or reading) dramatically improves both sleep onset and quality.

Waking up exhausted after a full night usually comes down to three culprits: poor sleep quality (not enough deep or REM sleep despite the hours), inconsistent sleep timing that confuses your circadian rhythm, or pre-sleep phone use that disrupts your natural melatonin cycle. More hours in bed is not the same as better sleep. The NextRoutine Sleep Reset focuses on sleep quality — particularly protecting the wind-down window — rather than just duration.

Phone screens emit blue light at the 480nm wavelength, which directly inhibits the pineal gland's production of melatonin. Even 30 minutes of screen use at full brightness can delay sleep onset by 45–60 minutes and reduce deep sleep by up to 22%. Social media specifically activates the stress response through comparison and novelty-seeking, keeping cortisol elevated long after you put the phone down. This is why a phone boundary — not just blue light glasses — is the foundation of the Sleep Reset plan.

An effective adult bedtime routine takes 20–30 minutes and follows three phases: dimming (lower lights and stop screens 60 minutes before sleep), winding (light activity like stretching, reading, or journalling for 20 minutes), and anchoring (same bedtime ± 30 minutes every night). The most important element is not what you do, but when you stop. Stopping screens is the non-negotiable foundation that everything else builds on.
