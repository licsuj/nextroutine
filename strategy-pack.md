Title: What I wish someone had told me about mornings before the baby arrived

I've got a 14-month-old. Here's the single most useful thing I learned in the first year, and I wish I'd known it before she arrived.

Your body produces a cortisol spike in the first 20–30 minutes after waking. It's called the cortisol awakening response (CAR). It's basically your natural activation system — drives alertness, focus, and motivation for the first half of the day.

When you check your phone first thing (which I did every morning for years), you hand that spike to reactive input. Someone else's agenda captures it. That's why mornings feel scattered even when you "have time."

The fix is stupidly simple: phone in another room for the first 20 minutes. Glass of water. One sentence in a notebook answering "what matters today?" That's it. Under 5 minutes.

I've been doing this for 400+ days now. On my worst nights (up 3x, maybe 3.5 hours of sleep), this still works because it's designed for the worst day, not the best.

I ended up building a whole system around this — it's called NextRoutine (nextroutine.com) — free newsletter with a protocol like this every week. But honestly, just the phone thing alone is worth trying tonight.

**Reddit — r/productivity**

Title: The morning routine problem nobody talks about: what happens when your circumstances change

Most productivity advice has a hidden assumption: stable sleep, predictable mornings, and control over your environment.

When those conditions change — new job with different hours, new baby, health issue, move to a new city — the routine breaks. And most people interpret that as a personal failure. "I need more discipline." No. You need a different system.

I went through this when my first child was born. Every morning routine I'd built in a decade collapsed. Not because the routines were bad, but because they assumed 8 hours of sleep and an empty house.

What I learned building a system that actually survived:

1. Design for the worst day, not the best. Your routine needs a full version (15 min), a fallback (5 min), and a minimum viable version (2 min). The range between your best and worst day determines the design constraints.

2. Fewer decisions = more durability. On low sleep, every choice costs more. The routine should be specific enough that you don't think — you follow the sequence.

3. The first 20 minutes matter most. The cortisol awakening response (CAR) is a 50–75% cortisol spike in the first 20–30 min after waking. It drives focus and motivation for the first half of the day. Protecting those 20 minutes (no phone, no reactive input) is the single highest-leverage habit change you can make.

I ended up building this into a system called NextRoutine (nextroutine.com). It's designed for new dads specifically, but the principles apply to anyone whose circumstances have pulled the rug out from under their routine.

What's your experience with routines breaking when life changes?
