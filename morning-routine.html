The science

What a morning routine actually does to your brain.

In the first 20–30 minutes after waking, your brain undergoes a surge of cortisol — the cortisol awakening response (CAR). This is not stress cortisol. It is the neurochemical signal that governs alertness, mood regulation, and cognitive readiness for the day ahead.

The CAR peaks within 20–30 minutes and determines your baseline functioning for the next 12–14 hours. What you do in this window shapes the neurological platform you operate on all day. This is not a metaphor — it is measurable.

The problem: checking your phone in the first 20 minutes hijacks this response entirely. You replace an internally-directed cortisol surge — one primed for proactive action — with a reactive, externally-driven one. The morning feels off before it has started. And as a new dad on broken sleep, you cannot afford to lose the 23-minute window that actually works in your favour.

Movement in the first 30 minutes activates the prefrontal cortex, the region responsible for planning, emotion regulation, and decision-making — the three things most under pressure in new fatherhood. Even five minutes of bodyweight exercise is enough to trigger this activation.

What goes wrong

The three mistakes that kill morning routines.

Not lack of discipline. These three design errors. Fix them before you start.

01
Mechanism: hijacked CAR

Starting with your phone.

Checking your phone within the first 20 minutes replaces the cortisol awakening response — your brain's natural alertness window — with reactive, externally-driven input. The morning feels off because neurologically, it is.

The fix
Charge your phone in another room. Put a physical notebook and pen next to your bed instead. The phone isn't a banned object — it's just not a first-20-minutes object.
02
Mechanism: optimism bias

Designing for your best-case morning.

Most people design their morning routine for a morning where they slept eight hours, nothing urgent came up, and they feel motivated. New dads rarely have that morning. When the routine is designed for optimal conditions, it collapses under real ones.

The fix
Design for your worst-case morning. If it works on three hours of sleep after a bad night, it works on everything else too. Start with the 10-minute version.
03
Mechanism: friction load

Ignoring environment design.

Willpower is finite and depleted fastest under sleep deprivation. A morning routine that requires multiple decisions before it starts — where are my shoes, what should I do first — will fail under pressure. Friction compounds when you're tired.

The fix
Lay out your clothes the night before. Have your journal already open to a blank page. Make the first three steps of your routine require zero decisions.
