The real problem

Why your morning routine keeps failing.

It's not willpower. It's architecture. Most morning routines are built for people with no children, no time constraints, and no one screaming at 2am.

It asks for too much time

A 90-minute morning routine is a fantasy when you have a newborn. The protocol must shrink to fit the life, not the other way around. Ours starts at 15 minutes and expands as your child sleeps longer.

The science

What actually happens in the first 23 minutes.

Your cortisol peaks 20–30 minutes after waking — this is your biological window for alertness, focus, and decision-making. Most fathers hand this window to social media. The NextRoutine protocol gives you a specific sequence to use it instead.

The 23-minute rule isn't arbitrary. It's the average time for cortisol to peak post-waking. Everything in the morning protocol is designed to work within this window — before the fog of the day takes hold.

The 15-Minute Protocol
1
Minutes 0–5: No phone
Water, light, one conscious breath. Build the cortisol window intentionally.
2
Minutes 5–12: Movement
7 minutes. Can be stretching. Can be walking. The threshold for neurological benefit is low — you just need to move.
3
Minutes 12–15: Set one intention
Not a to-do list. One sentence: what does a good day look like today? Write it or say it. Done.

Morning routine questions.

15 minutes is all you need in the newborn stage. The NextRoutine protocol works in three 5-minute blocks and is designed to work on four hours of broken sleep. As your child sleeps longer, the protocol scales — you simply add one block at a time.

Start 6–8 weeks before your due date. This gives you enough time to lock in the habit before the disruption begins. The pre-birth audit in the Fatherhood Starter Pack identifies exactly which habits to protect and which to rebuild from scratch.

Waking before the baby and spending 5 minutes in silence — no phone, no news. This creates a psychological buffer that affects every decision you make that morning. It also signals to your brain that you are a person with agency, not someone being controlled by their environment.

Lower the bar dramatically. A 15-minute routine done every day beats a 60-minute routine abandoned in week two. Identify one anchor habit — the non-negotiable first action — and build around it. Everything else can flex. The anchor cannot.
