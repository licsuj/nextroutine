Newborn Weeks 2–3: Build a Simple Dad Routine Without Overthinking

By weeks two and three, the first shock may fade and the tiredness may settle in. This is where tiny routines start to matter more than motivation.

J
Justin
Founder, NextRoutine

Choose a night role you can repeat

You may not be able to solve sleep, but you can own a part of the night: diaper changes, settling, bottle prep, burping, logging, or the morning reset.

The point of picking one role is removing the negotiation. At 3am, neither of you should be deciding who handles what. If you've already decided that you do diaper changes and the first settling, you save the cognitive cost of the decision in the moment when you have the least cognitive bandwidth. The role can change after a week. The structure matters more than which structure.

Create a daily handoff

A five-minute check-in can prevent resentment: what was hardest, what needs restocking, what does your partner need, and what can wait?

The handoff is not a status meeting. It's a chance to surface the things that won't get said otherwise — the hour that was harder than usual, the bottle of paracetamol that's running low, the friend who keeps texting that one of you needs to handle. Five minutes a day prevents fifty minutes of conflict on a Sunday afternoon when everyone is exhausted.

Make bonding ordinary

Bonding does not need to be dramatic. Repeat a walk, song, morning hold, bath routine, or burp-and-reset moment until it feels like yours.

The myth of the magical bonding moment is mostly a myth. Bonding is repetition. The thing you do every morning at 6am — the same hold, the same window, the same view — is what becomes "yours" with the baby. Pick one. Do it consistently. The feeling follows the action, not the other way around.

Lower the decision load

When everyone is tired, fewer choices help. Keep meals simple, supplies visible, and the next household reset obvious.

Decision fatigue is real and compounds with sleep deprivation. Reduce the menu to three breakfasts you both know how to make on autopilot. Keep nappies and wipes within reach of every change point in the house. Put the next thing that needs doing in plain sight. The goal is to remove every micro-decision that doesn't actually matter, so the decisions that do matter still get your attention.

Key takeaways

  • Pick one repeatable night role.
  • Use a short daily handoff with your partner.
  • Build one ordinary bonding ritual.
  • Lower the decision load wherever you can.
