How to Prepare for Fatherhood: 7 Things to Do Before the Baby Arrives

12 min read · April 2026

The nine months of pregnancy are not waiting time. They are the only preparation window you will ever have.

Most expectant fathers spend this period assembling furniture, reading a few chapters of a parenting book they never finish, and hoping for the best. Then the baby arrives, sleep disappears, and they discover that their mornings, their relationships, and their identity have been restructured without their consent.

This is not a list of nursery essentials or hospital bag checklists. This is about preparing you — your habits, your routines, and your readiness for the biggest transition of your adult life.

1. Build a morning routine that survives on four hours of sleep

Your current morning routine will not survive the newborn weeks. If it requires 60 uninterrupted minutes, a gym, or 8 hours of sleep — it will collapse by week two.

Build it now, while you have full sleep. Start with 15 minutes: water, one sentence in a notebook answering "what matters today?", and two minutes of movement. That is the full protocol. The key is that this routine has a 5-minute fallback version and a 2-minute minimum viable version.

The 2-minute version exists for the morning after three wake-ups. Water and one sentence. That counts. That is a day your routine held. The accumulation of those 2-minute days is what builds the habit — because those are the days you almost skipped entirely, and did not.

2. Fix your sleep architecture before it gets disrupted

Sleep runs in 90-minute cycles. If you wake up mid-cycle, you feel worse than if you had slept less but woken at a cycle boundary. This is called sleep inertia, and it explains why 8 hours can leave you exhausted while 6 hours can leave you sharp.

Start timing your sleep now. Count backwards from your wake time in 90-minute blocks. Go to bed at the cycle boundary, not when you "feel tired." Establish this habit before the baby disrupts everything — because when you are getting fragmented sleep, knowing where you are in the cycle becomes your most powerful tool.

Also: move your phone charger to the kitchen tonight. Blue light in the hour before sleep delays melatonin production. The single most effective sleep improvement most fathers can make is removing the phone from the bedroom.

3. Have the night feed conversation now

This is the conversation nobody wants to have and everybody needs to have.

Who gets up at 2am? Who gets up at 4am? Do you split the night in half, alternate nights, or take weekday/weekend shifts? What happens when one of you has an important meeting the next day?

Having this conversation at 3am with a screaming baby is a guarantee of resentment. Having it now, calmly, with a plan you both agree on, is preparation. Write it down. Not as a contract — as a starting point that you both revisit after week two when reality has adjusted the theory.

4. Audit your screen time

Check your screen time right now. The number is almost certainly higher than you think.

Here is why this matters for fatherhood: the hours you currently spend scrolling are the hours you will lose to childcare. If your current screen time is 4 hours per day and your baby requires 4 hours of direct attention, something else does not give — the phone does.

Start the reduction now, while it is a choice rather than a crisis. The goal is not zero screen time — it is intentional screen time. Know when you are choosing to use your phone versus when the phone is choosing for you.

5. Build your support network before you need it

Most new fathers rely entirely on their partner for emotional support. That is unsustainable. When your partner is also sleep-deprived and recovering from childbirth, she cannot be your therapist, your cheerleader, and your co-parent simultaneously.

Identify 2–3 people you can call at 10pm when you feel overwhelmed. Ideally, at least one of them has been through this recently. Tell them now: "I might need to call you in a few months. Is that okay?" Nobody says no to that question.

6. Get your finances to a place that does not add anxiety

Financial stress compounds every other stress. If you are already anxious about money, adding a baby and potentially losing one income (temporarily or permanently) turns anxiety into crisis.

Three financial actions before the baby arrives: build a buffer of 3 months of essential expenses (not total income — just rent, food, utilities, insurance). Understand your parental leave entitlements and when they start. Review your insurance coverage and update beneficiaries.

This is not financial advice. It is anxiety reduction through preparation. The father who knows his family can survive financially for 3 months without panic makes better decisions at 4am than the father who is running mental calculations about the electricity bill while changing a nappy.

7. Decide who you want to be as a father — and write it down

This sounds abstract. It is the most practical thing on this list.

Write three sentences: "The father I want to be is..." "The habit that will define my fatherhood is..." "The one thing I will not compromise on is..."

Put these somewhere you will see them at 4am. Above the changing table. On the bathroom mirror. In a note on your phone (the phone that is now in the kitchen).

When you are exhausted, overwhelmed, and questioning everything — these three sentences are your anchor. They do not make the hard moments easier. They make the hard moments meaningful.

The preparation nobody offers you

The self-improvement world has thousands of resources for building habits, fixing sleep, and managing screen time. Almost none of them are written for fathers. They assume consistent sleep, predictable mornings, and a stable identity — three things that fatherhood removes simultaneously.

Start tonight. Move your phone to the kitchen. Write your three sentences. Do the 15-minute morning protocol tomorrow. You have months before the chaos. Use them.
