7 Habits Every New Dad Should Build Before the Baby Arrives

The nine months before your child's birth are the highest-leverage window you'll ever have. Most expectant fathers spend them waiting. Here's what to do instead — seven specific habits that compound through the first year and beyond.

JW
James Whitfield
Founder, NextRoutine

Most fatherhood advice starts at birth. The first few weeks of being a new dad, managing a newborn, the sleep deprivation, the identity shift — all of that is covered extensively, from parenting books to YouTube channels to well-meaning friends and family. What nobody talks about is the nine months before.

The expectant period — from the moment you find out to the birth — is the single most underutilised window in fatherhood. You have time. You have relative stability. You have the cognitive bandwidth to build new habits before the chaos restructures your entire life. Most expectant fathers spend this period in anticipation mode, buying things, reading about babies, maybe attending an NCT class or two. Almost none of them use it to actually build the habits that will carry them through.

The fathers who show up strongest in the first eight weeks of parenthood — who stay emotionally present, who maintain some structure, who don't fall apart at week three — are almost always the ones who started preparing their own routines and psychology before the birth. Not by reading more books about babies. By building the habits that make them better equipped to face whatever comes.

Here are the seven habits I'd tell every expectant father to build. In priority order.

Habit 1: The No-Phone Morning Window

Start your day without picking up your phone for the first ten minutes. That's it. No news, no social media, no email, no messages. Water, a few minutes outside, one written priority for the day — then you can have your phone back.

This sounds trivial. It is not. The first ten minutes of your day set the cognitive pattern for everything that follows. When you reach for your phone immediately on waking, you start the day in reactive mode — responding to information that was chosen by an algorithm, not by you. This reactive pattern tends to persist. By the time you have a newborn and your morning window is the only reliable time you have to yourself, this habit will be the difference between a day you chose and a day that happens to you.

Build it now, when stakes are lower. Ten minutes. Every morning. For 30 days.

Habit 2: Sleep Banking

Sleep banking — deliberately accumulating sleep in the weeks before a known period of sleep deprivation — is one of the few proactive strategies that works. Research from the Walter Reed Army Institute of Research shows that extended sleep in the days before sleep restriction significantly reduces cognitive impairment during the restriction period.

In practical terms, this means getting consistent 8–9 hour nights in the final trimester rather than catching up sporadically. Go to bed 45 minutes earlier than you normally would. Protect Sunday evenings. Treat your sleep as a strategic resource, because starting fatherhood well-rested gives you a larger physiological buffer than starting it in the usual semi-depleted state most adults walk around in.

"I slept 9 hours a night for the last six weeks of the pregnancy. People thought I was being selfish. By week four of the newborn phase, I was the only dad in our NCT group who hadn't had a complete breakdown." — Marcus H., NextRoutine member, Stage 1

Habit 3: The Partner Conversation Framework

The single biggest predictor of how well couples navigate the transition to parenthood is the quality of the conversations they have before the birth about how they'll handle it. Not conversations about nurseries or names — conversations about night shifts, about division of labour, about what you'll each need to stay functional, about what "support" actually means to each of you.

Most couples don't have these conversations. They assume they'll figure it out. They do figure it out — messily, under sleep deprivation, with resentment as the primary curriculum. The couples who navigate it best have a framework established in advance.

Habit 4: The Identity Audit

Fatherhood restructures your identity whether you engage with the restructuring consciously or not. The fathers who struggle most in the first year are usually the ones who were caught off guard by this — who expected to add "father" to their existing identity without anything shifting, and found that it doesn't work that way.

The Identity Audit is simple: write down, in detail, who you are now — your routines, your priorities, your relationships, your sense of yourself. Then answer honestly: which parts of this are incompatible with being a present father? Which parts do you want to preserve? Which parts were actually ready to be let go?

This exercise has no wrong answers. It just makes the transition conscious rather than reactive. Fathers who've done this tend to report feeling more prepared, not because they have a plan, but because they've already started the psychological work that others begin under duress.

Habit 5: A 15-Minute Movement Habit

Not a gym programme. Not a fitness goal. A fifteen-minute daily movement habit that requires nothing — no equipment, no facility, no optimal conditions. A walk. A bodyweight circuit. Ten minutes of yoga. Anything that gets you physically active and that you can do in your kitchen at 5:30am with a baby monitor on the counter.

The reason this matters is not fitness. It's baseline cortisol regulation, mood stability, and the psychological anchor of having one daily non-negotiable that belongs to you. Fathers who maintain physical movement habits through the newborn phase consistently report better emotional regulation, lower anxiety, and higher relationship satisfaction than those who don't — even though the movement itself is minimal.

Habit 6: Screen Time Awareness

Check your screen time right now. Add up your daily average. Now imagine that number going up by 40–60% after the birth, because that's what happens. The combination of exhaustion, reduced stimulation, and increased transition moments — times when you're sitting with a baby who doesn't need feeding but does need holding — creates ideal conditions for passive scrolling to become a default.

Building a replacement habit now — a book you keep near the sofa, a podcast loaded on your phone, a brief daily journaling practice — means the replacement is already in place when the trigger arrives. Trying to build it under sleep deprivation doesn't work.

Habit 7: A Weekly Review Practice

The Sunday Night Reset — 20 minutes at the end of each week to review what happened, set three priorities for the coming week, and prepare the next morning — is the single highest-leverage routine I know for maintaining direction under chaotic conditions. It works when you have a baby, when you have a deadline, when you've had a terrible week. Because it's not about everything going well. It's about maintaining the habit of reflection and intention regardless.

Build this now, before the birth. Run it for eight weeks during the pregnancy so it's automatic by the time the baby arrives. By month two of parenthood, when everything feels like it's happening to you rather than being chosen by you, you'll have a weekly reset that restores a sense of agency. That's more valuable than it sounds in the abstract.

The window closes at birth

None of these habits are complicated. All of them are achievable in the next four weeks. The reason most expectant fathers don't build them isn't lack of information — it's lack of urgency. The baby isn't here yet. There's time.

There isn't, really. The window is nine months long and then it closes. The fathers who walk through it having built these habits walk into parenthood as a better-prepared version of themselves. The ones who wait to "get back to it" after the birth are usually still waiting at month six.

Start tonight with Habit 1. Ten minutes. No phone. Let everything else follow.
