The science

The 90-minute cycle — and how to use it to wake up rested.

Human sleep doesn't work in continuous hours. It works in 90-minute cycles, each composed of four stages: light sleep (N1), deeper sleep (N2), deep slow-wave sleep (N3/SWS), and REM sleep. Your body moves through this sequence repeatedly throughout the night. Completing a full cycle is what leaves you feeling rested. Breaking one midway is what leaves you feeling wrecked despite enough time in bed.

This is why waking after eight hours can feel worse than waking after seven and a half. Eight hours doesn't align to a cycle boundary (8 ÷ 1.5 = 5.3 cycles). Seven and a half hours aligns perfectly (7.5 ÷ 1.5 = 5 complete cycles). The extra 30 minutes breaks a cycle. Your body wakes mid-REM, which produces the "sleep inertia" — the groggy, heavy-headed, difficult-to-function feeling that most people blame on not sleeping enough, when it's actually about where in the cycle they woke.

For new dads, this has a specific application: even with fragmented sleep, you can engineer your available sleep windows to align with cycle boundaries. If the baby feeds at 1am and you're back in bed by 1:30am, setting an alarm for 4:30am (3 cycles), 6am (3 cycles from a 1:30am sleep onset), or 7:30am works better than 8am.

The three killers

What's actually destroying your sleep quality.

It's rarely the total hours. It's these three mechanisms — and all three can be addressed without changing when you go to bed.

Killer 01

Screens at night — it's not the light, it's the arousal

Blue light from screens suppresses melatonin production by up to 50% for up to three hours after exposure. That's the well-known issue. The less-discussed problem is emotional arousal: reading news, scrolling social media, and checking work email activates the sympathetic nervous system — fight-or-flight — which is physiologically incompatible with sleep onset. Even if you fall asleep quickly after scrolling, the architecture of your sleep changes: less time in N3 (deep sleep), more frequent arousals, and significantly lower sleep quality scores.

The mechanism: Amygdala activation from emotionally charged content increases cortisol and adrenaline. These hormones have a 45–90 minute half-life. Putting your phone down at midnight means you're still physiologically aroused at 1am, even if your eyes are closed.
Killer 02

Inconsistent schedule — social jet lag

Your circadian rhythm is set by light exposure and — critically — by the consistency of your wake time. The research on "social jet lag" shows that varying your wake time by more than one hour between weekdays and weekends produces cognitive impairment equivalent to crossing multiple time zones. For new dads, the temptation to "sleep in" on non-work mornings actively undermines the sleep quality of the following three nights.

The mechanism: Your suprachiasmatic nucleus (the circadian pacemaker) anchors to your wake time, not your bedtime. A consistent wake time — even after a poor night — resets the clock and makes the following night's sleep significantly better. Sleeping in delays melatonin onset the next evening, making it harder to fall asleep and continuing the cycle.
Killer 03

Caffeine timing — the adenosine debt you don't know you're building

Caffeine does not give you energy. It blocks adenosine receptors — the receptors that signal sleepiness to your brain. While caffeine is blocking those receptors, adenosine continues accumulating. When caffeine's half-life ends (approximately 5–7 hours), the accumulated adenosine floods back in, producing the post-afternoon energy crash most people experience. More importantly: caffeine consumed after 2pm still has significant adenosine-blocking activity during the first half of your sleep window, reducing deep sleep by up to 20%.

The mechanism: A cup of coffee at 3pm has roughly 50% of its caffeine still active at 8pm. At midnight it still has 25% activity. The result is lighter sleep architecture and less restorative N3 sleep — even if you fall asleep fine. Cut caffeine by 2pm and most people report meaningfully deeper sleep within 3–5 days.
Tonight-applicable

The 3-2-1 wind-down protocol.

Three hours. Three rules. Applied consistently, this protocol changes the quality of your sleep within a week — without changing when you go to bed or how long you sleep.

3
3 hours before bed
Stop eating

Active digestion raises core body temperature. Sleep onset requires a drop in core temperature of approximately 1°C. Eating within three hours of bed — particularly carbohydrates and alcohol — delays this temperature drop and delays sleep onset, even if you feel full and sleepy. For a 10:30pm target bedtime, dinner at 7:30pm. For 11pm, 8pm. Adjust and hold the boundary.

2
2 hours before bed
Stop working and intense thinking

Cognitive work — problem-solving, email, planning — activates prefrontal cortex activity that is physiologically incompatible with sleep onset. The brain cannot transition from "solving problems" to "sleeping" in twenty minutes. It needs a decompression window. Two hours before bed, the day's work is done. What didn't happen today will wait. Tomorrow's problems are not your concern until tomorrow.

1
1 hour before bed
Stop screens entirely

One hour before your target sleep time, screens go off. Phone in another room (or on silent, face down). TV off. Laptop closed. Replace with: a warm shower (raises then drops core temperature, accelerating sleep onset), reading physical books, light conversation with your partner, or simple breathwork. This one-hour window is the highest-leverage sleep intervention available — and it costs nothing.

FAQ

Sleep questions — answered.

The most effective strategy is circadian anchoring: waking at the same time every day regardless of night-feed schedule. Combined with a consistent wind-down protocol — screens off 60 minutes before bed, a fixed pre-sleep sequence, and a cool, dark bedroom — most new dads report measurable improvement in sleep quality within 7 days without changing total sleep hours.

You may be waking mid-cycle. Human sleep runs in 90-minute cycles. Waking in the middle of a cycle produces more grogginess than waking at a cycle boundary. Try setting your alarm for 7.5 hours after you fall asleep (5 complete cycles) instead of 8. You may feel significantly more rested despite less total time in bed.

A sleep routine is a consistent sequence of behaviours performed before bed that signals to your nervous system that sleep is approaching. Its effectiveness comes from conditioning — the same sequence, repeated nightly, becomes a reliable trigger for melatonin production and nervous system downregulation. The content of the routine matters less than its consistency.

Blue light from screens suppresses melatonin production by up to 50% for up to three hours after exposure. But the more significant mechanism is emotional arousal: scrolling social media, news, and email keeps your brain in a high-alert state that is physiologically incompatible with sleep onset. The emotional content of screens — not just the light — is the primary sleep disruptor for most adults.

The 3-2-1 wind-down rule: 3 hours before bed, stop eating; 2 hours before bed, stop work and intense mental activity; 1 hour before bed, stop screens entirely. Applied consistently, it creates a predictable nervous system downregulation window that allows melatonin production to begin on schedule and makes sleep onset significantly easier. Most people notice a difference within the first week.
