The science

Sleep in 90-minute cycles. Wake at the right time.

Sleep is not a uniform state. It progresses through four distinct stages in approximately 90-minute cycles: light sleep (N1 and N2), deep slow-wave sleep (N3), and REM sleep. A full night of 8 hours contains roughly five of these cycles.

The key insight: waking mid-cycle — particularly during deep sleep — causes sleep inertia. That heavy, exhausted, dragged-out-of-a-coma feeling that persists for 30–60 minutes. It is not about sleep quantity. It is about where in the cycle you wake up.

A 7.5-hour sleep (five complete cycles) will consistently leave you more rested than an 8-hour sleep that interrupts the fifth cycle in deep sleep. This is the number most dads never know.

What to eliminate

The three sleep killers.

Before any protocol, eliminate these. Each one is undermining the sleep you are getting.

Screens before bed.

You probably know screens affect sleep. Here is the mechanism most people don't know: blue light from phone and laptop screens suppresses melatonin production by up to 50% when used in the 90 minutes before bed.

The mechanismMelatonin is the chemical signal that tells your body it is time to sleep. Blue light mimics daylight, signalling to the suprachiasmatic nucleus that it is still daytime. Production is suppressed. Sleep onset is delayed. Sleep quality is reduced — even if you fall asleep on time.
The fix
No screens 60–90 minutes before sleep. Not just the phone — laptop, TV, all of it. If this feels impossible, start with 20 minutes and extend weekly.

Inconsistent sleep schedule.

Sleeping in on weekends, staying up late on some nights and early on others — this creates what researchers call social jet lag: a chronic misalignment between your circadian rhythm and your actual sleep schedule. Most people with poor sleep quality have this as the root cause, not the hours they're getting.

The mechanismYour circadian rhythm is regulated primarily by your wake time, not your bedtime. Every time you sleep in by 90+ minutes, you effectively shift your body clock forward — like flying east across time zones. The resulting misalignment degrades sleep quality and daytime alertness independently of total sleep duration.
The fix
Keep your wake time consistent within 30 minutes — even on weekends, even after bad nights. The bedtime can flex. The wake time cannot.

Caffeine timing.

A coffee at 2pm is still significantly affecting your sleep at 10pm. Caffeine's half-life is 5–7 hours in most adults. This means half of a 200mg dose (a standard espresso) is still circulating in your bloodstream when you try to sleep six hours later — blocking the adenosine receptors that drive sleep pressure.

The mechanismAdenosine is a chemical that accumulates while you are awake and drives sleep pressure — the feeling of being sleepy. Caffeine works by blocking adenosine receptors, not by producing alertness. While it is blocking those receptors, adenosine continues to accumulate. When caffeine clears, the full force of accumulated adenosine hits — which is why the afternoon crash often feels worse than the original tiredness.
The fix
Set a hard caffeine cutoff at 1–2pm. A single espresso at 7am has cleared by 1pm. An afternoon coffee at 3pm is still active at 9pm.
Tonight-applicable

The 3-2-1 wind-down protocol.

Three simple rules that give your nervous system a predictable signal that sleep is coming. Not rigid — consistent. The goal is to associate these cues with sleep so strongly that your body begins preparing for it automatically.

3hbefore bed
Do tonight

No food or alcohol.

Digestion raises core body temperature. Sleep onset requires a drop in core temperature of 1–2°C. Eating late delays this drop and fragments the first sleep cycles. Alcohol — despite feeling sedating — suppresses REM sleep and causes early morning waking.

2hbefore bed
Do tonight

No more screens.

Phone, laptop, TV — all off. The living room overhead light also counts: LED ceiling lights emit significant blue light. Switch to lamps. If you must use a screen, enable Night Mode or f.lux, and reduce brightness to minimum.

1hbefore bed
Do tonight

Begin your wind-down routine.

The same activities, in the same order, every night. Low light. Low stimulation. Options that work: a warm shower (the subsequent cool-down triggers sleep onset), reading a physical book, a brief journal entry, or slow stretching. What it is matters less than doing the same thing every night.
