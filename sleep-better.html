The real cause

Your sleep isn't the problem. What you do before sleep is.

Most people try to fix sleep by adjusting when they go to bed. That's wrong. The fix happens 45 minutes earlier.

Screen exposure wrecks melatonin

Phone and laptop screens emit blue light that suppresses melatonin production for up to 3 hours after exposure. You're telling your brain it's noon at 10pm — then wondering why you can't sleep deeply.

Cortisol spikes from late-night content

News, social media, and high-stimulus content spike cortisol in the evening. You fall asleep but your body never properly recovers. The quality of your sleep is set by what you consume in the final hour.

Inconsistent timing destroys your rhythm

Your circadian rhythm is calibrated to a consistent wake time more than a consistent sleep time. Varying your wake time by even 45 minutes across the week can halve the restorative quality of your sleep.

The 30-minute fix

The wind-down that changes everything.

Thirty minutes of intentional wind-down — done consistently — improves deep sleep architecture within 7 days. That's not a marketing claim. It's documented sleep science. The protocol below is what that looks like in practice for a father with a newborn or toddler.

The key mechanism: you are not trying to force sleep. You are creating the conditions under which sleep will happen naturally. Darkness, low stimulation, and a consistent sequence of actions signal your nervous system that the threat-assessment phase of the day is over.

The 30-Minute Wind-Down
10:00
Phone down. Lights dim.
Every screen off or in a different room. No exceptions.
10:05
Physical reset. 10 minutes.
Light stretching or walking. Releases physical tension from the day.
10:20
Brain dump. 5 minutes.
Write whatever is in your head. Not journaling — just clearing. This stops 2am thought spirals.
10:25
Read. 5 minutes.
Paper only. Fiction preferred — not self-development. Your brain needs permission to stop solving.
10:30
Sleep.
Same time. Every night. Including weekends.

Sleep questions answered.

Sleep quality matters more than sleep quantity. The most common cause is destroying your sleep architecture in the hour before bed — phone screens suppressing melatonin, cortisol spikes from late-night content, irregular sleep timing. The wind-down protocol addresses all three simultaneously.

Focus on sleep quality not quantity — you can't control when the baby wakes, but you can protect the sleep you do get. The most important change: put your phone down 45 minutes before you intend to sleep, even if you're only getting 4 hours. Quality sleep on 4 hours beats poor sleep on 7.

Sleep banking is consistently getting 8–9 hours of sleep in the 3–4 weeks before birth to build a buffer. Research suggests banked sleep provides modest protection against the cognitive effects of early sleep deprivation — and it's one of the most practical things an expectant father can do.
