The real problem

You're not broken. Your pre-sleep routine is.

Three things that happen before you get into bed are destroying your sleep quality — none of them have anything to do with how tired you are.

📱

Your phone is wrecking your melatonin

Blue light from screens tells your brain it's still 2pm. Melatonin production — the chemical that actually makes you sleepy — gets suppressed for 60–90 minutes after you put the phone down. You're lying in bed wondering why you can't sleep. The phone made sure you wouldn't be able to.

🧠

Your brain is still activated when your body is in bed

Scrolling social media, reading news, or checking messages in the hour before bed keeps your threat-detection systems online. Even after you put the phone down, cortisol stays elevated. Your body is in bed. Your nervous system is still at the office.

🔁

You have no wind-down signal — so your body never starts

Your circadian rhythm needs consistent timing signals to anchor your sleep cycle. Without a consistent wind-down routine — same activities, same sequence, same time — your brain never gets the signal that sleep is coming. You don't wind down. You just stop being awake.

The science

The 30-minute wind-down that changes everything

You don't need a 2-hour sleep preparation ritual. You need three consistent signals delivered in the same sequence, at the same time, every night. That's what the NextRoutine Sleep Reset gives you.

1

The Digital Cutoff (60 minutes before bed)

Not "reduce screen time" — a specific cutoff. Phone face-down in another room. The timing matters because melatonin suppression peaks 60 minutes after exposure, so a 60-minute cutoff gives your body exactly the window it needs.

2

The Temperature Drop Protocol (30 minutes before bed)

Core body temperature must drop 1–2°C to initiate deep sleep. A warm shower or bath 30 minutes before bed accelerates this drop through vasodilation. Counterintuitive — but one of the most consistent findings in sleep research.

3

The Cognitive Offload (10 minutes before bed)

Write down everything your brain thinks you'll forget overnight. The "unfinished business" that activates the Zeigarnik effect at 2am. Paper, not phone. Getting it out of your head removes the loop that keeps overthinkers awake.

Common questions

Sleep questions, answered without the usual vague advice

Waking up exhausted after 8 hours usually means you're not reaching deep sleep stages — not that you need more hours. The two most common causes: blue light from screens before bed suppresses melatonin by up to 50%, meaning your brain can't fully transition into deep sleep; and inconsistent sleep timing, which disrupts your circadian rhythm's ability to time-lock your sleep cycles correctly. More hours won't fix either problem. A structured wind-down routine that removes screens and stabilises timing will.
Most people feel a meaningful shift within 7–10 days of consistently applying a proper wind-down routine. The circadian rhythm responds relatively quickly to consistent timing signals — going to bed and waking at the same time every day (including weekends) for two weeks is typically enough to re-anchor your internal clock. The subjective feeling of "better sleep" usually arrives before the objective improvement in sleep quality by a few days.
Yes — and more significantly than most people realise. Blue light from phone and laptop screens suppresses melatonin production by 50% when used in the 2–3 hours before bed. But screen time doesn't just affect melatonin — the psychological arousal from social media, news, and messages also elevates cortisol and activates the brain's threat-detection systems, making it harder to fall asleep even after you've put the device down. The NextRoutine wind-down addresses both the light and the psychological activation.
The most effective wind-down routines share three elements: a consistent start time (same time every night, even weekends), a digital cutoff at least 60 minutes before bed, and a physical cue that signals sleep to your body (temperature drop, darkness, a consistent pre-sleep behaviour). The NextRoutine Sunday Night Reset addresses all three and adds a pre-week anxiety-clearing practice that removes the cognitive activation that keeps overthinkers awake.
Willpower alone won't stop bedtime scrolling because you're up against habit and design. The phone is in your hand because it's always been in your hand at that time. The apps are designed to make stopping feel like loss. The fix is a replacement sequence, not restriction: the Doom-Scroll Detox uses a physical object replacement, a deliberate 15-minute intentional screen window, and a closing ritual that makes stopping feel satisfying. By day 5, most people stop reaching for the phone without conscious effort.
