Stage 2 · Weeks 1–8
Survive and stay present.
Sleep deprivation is measurable. In the first eight weeks, new parents lose an average of 44 nights of sleep — equivalent to two months of impairment. This is not a willpower challenge. It's a logistics problem. The 15-minute new dad morning protocol was built specifically for this window: it works when you're running on three hours, when the baby won't settle, and when you haven't had a warm cup of coffee in a week.

Stage 3 · Months 3–12
Rebuild your routine.
By month three, the chaos begins to have a shape. The baby sleeps in longer stretches. You know what the day roughly looks like. This is the window to rebuild — not to the routine you had before (that's gone), but to a routine that fits who you are now. Career, body, relationship, ambition: all of these need to be renegotiated. This stage gives you the framework for that negotiation.

Stage 4 · Year 1+
Become the model.
Your child learns what a morning looks like by watching you have one. They learn what discipline looks like by watching you practise it. They learn whether adults take care of themselves — or just manage — by watching you every single day. The habits you build in the first year of fatherhood are the standard your child grows up thinking is normal. That's not pressure. That's the best reason there is to build them well.

Frequently asked questions.

The moment you find out. The nine months of pregnancy are the single best window you will ever have to build habits before your life is restructured. Dads who start before the birth consistently report a smoother transition than those who wait until after. The pre-birth audit takes 20 minutes and immediately shows you which habits will break first.

In the first eight weeks, it's 15 minutes: 5 minutes of movement (not exercise — movement), 5 minutes on one priority task, 5 minutes of a short journal prompt. Not a 5am ice bath. Not a 90-minute productivity ritual. A realistic sequence that survives on four hours of broken sleep. As your child grows and sleep improves, the protocol expands with you — to 30 minutes at Stage 3 and a full routine by year one.

Parenting books cover what happens to the baby. Parenting blogs are for mothers, or they're written as if all fathers are clueless. NextRoutine is built specifically for the father's experience — your sleep, your identity shift, your morning routine, your career rebalancing, your relationship under pressure. It's the first platform that treats the father as the primary audience, not an afterthought. Every protocol is mechanism-based: we explain why something works, not just what to do.

Yes — and the pre-birth audit explicitly covers partner alignment. The goal is not two competing individual routines but a household rhythm that supports both parents. The audit identifies overlap and conflict points before the baby arrives. The Stage 3 framework explicitly covers relationship rebuilding — the first year changes the dynamic of every partnership and the framework addresses that directly.
