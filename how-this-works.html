How NextRoutine actually works.

A note on methodology, because most "expert" content sites don't tell you how their content gets made. This one does.

The honest disclosure

NextRoutine is built by one person — Justin, founder, software engineer, first-time father based in Malta. I use AI as a research and drafting assistant. Every article is reviewed, edited, and approved by me before it ships. Nothing gets published unread.

This is the same approach a journalist uses with a research assistant — the assistant accelerates the work, the human owns the output. The difference is most sites don't tell you they do this. I am.

What "research-based" means here

Each protocol cites specific sources where claims are made. If you see "research suggests X," there's a link to the actual study. If a study has been overstated in the popular press, I note that. If two studies disagree, I say so.

When I write "in my own testing," I mean I personally tried it. When I write "research suggests," I mean someone else tested it and the citation is below.

I do not write "fathers in our community report" unless that community exists at the size implied. As the newsletter and Pro membership grow, that language will start to honestly appear.

What this site is not

Not medical advice. Sleep, anxiety, and mental health protocols here are research-based but not a substitute for talking to your GP. If you're experiencing severe sleep issues or symptoms of postpartum depression (yes, fathers get this too), please speak to a healthcare professional.

Not parenting advice for the child. The protocols are for the father. Your child has their own needs and you should rely on their paediatrician for those.

Not an evidence base for major decisions. If you're making a big financial or career choice around fatherhood, get specialised advice.

The changelog

This site updates. Protocols evolve. When a piece of advice gets re-tested and refined, the article gets updated and a note added.

Contact

hello@nextroutine.com goes to my inbox. I read everything. I reply to most things. If something in a protocol didn't work for you, I want to know — that's how the next version gets better.

— Justin
